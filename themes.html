Issues and themes in the mural debate

The call to reform art came early in the 20th century, and central to issues of reform was the audience. America had experienced a previous “mural renaissance” during the late 19th century, producing works with complex allegorical programs steeped in a European tradition. Artists like Kenyon Cox, John Singer Sargent, and Edwin Blashfield, had completed large scale mural programs at many public institutions such as the Library of Congress and the Boston Public Library. These mural series functioned as forms of civic education. Influenced by theorists like Benedict Anderson and his “imaged community”, progressive era muralists used murals to reinforce ideals of national identity, decorum, and taste. Grand allegorical figures and references to antiquity were used to educate and elevate the aesthetic taste of the general public, but for those not educated in the classics and arts these murals could be more confusing than enlightening.

By the 1920s these murals were being to be seen as antiquated and out of touch with the everyday American. American journals such The New Masses, and The Liberator where some of the first to begin calling for a reform in American art. Men such as theorist Lewis Mumford and artist Hugo Gellert were just some of the vocal critics of the decadence and exclusivity of late 19th and early 20th century American art. They championed a new genre of art they name "Proletarian Art" art for the average man. Simultaneously Mexican artists were developing their own reaction to European Classicism. In the effort to reform art both movements focused on audience, subject, and style.

  • AudienceOpen or Close

    Virginia Marquardt sums up the basic tenets of proletarian art as “the self-deprecating demeanor of the unschooled critics; the unsophisticated worker viewed as a genuine appreciator of art; and the elevation of direct, forthright character as the fundamental criterion of art.”1 Also present in the discourse of proletarian art is a strong anti-bourgeois sentiment and the introduction of the artist as propagandist. Proletarian art was made for the people and had to be accesible to the people. Public art, like murals and widely circulated prints, became the most celebreated form of proletarian art.

    Proletarian art is heavily influenced by Leninist-Marxism. Lenin equated the artist to a cog in the great machine of society. Only when art was fully integrated into society, made by the worker for the worker, could it be true art.

    Art belongs to the people. It must let its roots go down deep into the very thick of the laboring masses. It should be understood and loved by these masses. It must unite and elevate their feelings, thoughts and will. It must awaken and develop the artistic instinct within them. Must we serve sweet cakes to a small minority while the workers and the peasants are in need of black bread?Vladimir Lenin, Marxism and Art

    While American theorists were debating whom art should cater to, David Alfaro Siqueiros and Diego Rivera were also debating what art should be and whom it should serve. In 1919, Siqueiros received a government grant to study art in Europe in recognition of his good military service during the Mexican revolution. In Paris he met Rivera, who at the time was heavily involved with the avant-garde cubists. Siqueiros found the formal experimentation of the European avant-garde lacking in substance. He criticized the artists for being too concerned with “aesthetic trivialities,” and could not find a “common philosophical or political ground with the French avant-garde.”2

    Siqueiros and Rivera made their way to Italy where they were particularly taken with the murals of the Italian Renaissance. The public nature of the murals and their function of educating through imagery fulfilled the need for the humanity that Siqueiros felt was missing in the work of the French avant-garde. It was also in Europe where Siqueiros was introduced to Leninist-Marxist ideology, which would serve as the main influence of his overall artistic philosophy. Under the influence of Lenin and the Italian masters, Siqueiros and Rivera theorized that public murals could be the great modern art form they were searching for.

  • StyleOpen or Close

    In 1921, Siqueiros published Three Appeals for the Current Guidance of the New Generation of American Painters and Sculptors in which he applies Leninist-Marxist theory to modern art in America. In the manifesto, he calls for art to “restore the lost values of painting and sculpture as well as endowing art with new values.”# A return to lost values was a direct critique of modernist abstraction which had become overly occupied with formal experimentation.

    Siqueiros's manifesto was extremely influence for both Mexican and American artists. In the manifesto Siqueiros encourages artists to ignore European influence and look to their own national traditions. Avant-garde "isms" were symptomatic of European decadence, to restore "the lost values of painting and sculpture" Siqueiros urges artists to adopt a more traditionally formalist style while displaying the spirit of the age. What results is a monumental style, filled with solid forms, dynamic angles and colors, clean lines, and the spirit of the "dynamic" age.

  • SubjectOpen or Close

    The ultimate goal of Siqueiros's manifesto was to create a universal art which connects with our present “dynamic age”, embracing the machine and construction, the things that make up “the contemporary aspects of our daily lives.”1 Murals in Mexico moved away from allegorical imagery and began to depict the scenes of everyday life after the Mexican Revolution. The peasant, the soldier, and the worker became the holy trinity of of mural iconography. In America, artists followed the Mexican lead depicting current events, scenes of industry, and modern urban life.
